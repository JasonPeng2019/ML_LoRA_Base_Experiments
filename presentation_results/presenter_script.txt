--- SLIDE: EXPERIMENT 1 — SPECTRAL ENERGY OF FULL FINE-TUNING UPDATES ---

Our first follow-up experiment is SVD of a full fine-tuning update. We fully fine-tune a smaller
model — specifically RoBERTa-base on SST-2 — compute the true weight delta, and check whether its
singular values decay quickly. If most of the energy is in the top few singular values, that would
directly support the low-rank update hypothesis.

Our results for this are: on the plot, each faint blue line is one of the 24 attention query or
value matrices across RoBERTa's 12 layers, and the dark curve is the mean. Concretely: the top
single direction captures 14 percent of total update energy. The top 8 directions capture 38
percent. The top 32 directions capture 59 percent. And even using all 128 directions we looked at
only gets us to 83 percent — you need 107 out of 768 possible directions to explain 80 percent of
the energy.

These show that full fine-tuning updates are geometrically concentrated but not extremely low-rank.
The energy falls off steadily — there isn't a clean "elbow" at rank 8 or 16, but the top directions
do carry a disproportionate share of the signal. This is exactly the motivation LoRA relies on:
you don't need to update all 768 dimensions to make meaningful task progress; a small low-rank
correction to the most important directions is often enough.


--- SLIDE: EXPERIMENT 2 — SUBSPACE OVERLAP: LoRA vs. FULL FINE-TUNING ---

Second, we compared LoRA's learned subspace against the full fine-tuning subspace. If LoRA finds
the same top directions as full fine-tuning, that would show LoRA is not merely a cheap
approximation, but is actually capturing the important part of the full update.

We measured this using the overlap metric phi of k — the squared Frobenius norm of the cross-gram
matrix between the top-k left singular vectors of the full-FT delta and the LoRA checkpoint delta,
divided by k. A score of 1 would mean the subspaces are identical; the random-subspace baseline
is k divided by 768.

We had these results: at k equals 1, the mean overlap is 0.047, which is 36 times the random
baseline. At k equals 16, the overlap is 0.109, about 5 times random. At k equals 64, it's 0.123,
about 1.5 times random. The best single matrix at k equals 1 reaches 0.259. These values were
computed over all 24 attention query and value matrices.

These show that LoRA and full fine-tuning do not learn the same subspace — the maximum overlap we
see is around 12 to 13 percent — but they are significantly more aligned than random, especially
at small k. The single most important update direction is 36 times more shared than chance would
predict. As k grows, the enrichment shrinks: this makes sense because LoRA only uses 8 rank
dimensions while full fine-tuning spreads energy across over a hundred directions. So LoRA isn't
just memorizing noise — it's finding the same dominant task-relevant direction, just without the
long tail.


--- SLIDE: EXPERIMENT 3 — MIXTURE OF LoRA EXPERTS (ORACLE ROUTING) ---

Third, we tested mixtures of LoRA modules. The question is whether several task-specific LoRA
modules can be combined to cover a more general model. Concretely, we take one frozen RoBERTa-base,
keep a pool of six task-specific LoRA adapters — one for each GLUE task — and use an oracle router
that sends each example to the adapter that matches its task. We evaluate this oracle-routed mixture
against running each LoRA adapter standalone, and against full fine-tuned models evaluated on their
own task.

For this experiment, we got: on SST-2, oracle LoRA and per-task LoRA both score 94.3 percent versus
full FT at 95.2 percent — a 0.9 point gap. On MRPC, LoRA scores 87.1 percent versus FFT at 91.1
percent. On RTE, LoRA scores 71.8 percent while full fine-tuning only reaches 56.3 percent —
that's a 15.5 percentage point advantage for LoRA. On the large tasks MNLI and QNLI, the two
methods are nearly tied within half a point. On QQP, LoRA scores 88.0 versus FFT at 90.2. Across
all six tasks, the macro average is 86.9 percent for LoRA versus 85.4 percent for FFT. The weighted
average — dominated by the large MNLI, QNLI, and QQP sets — is nearly tied: 89.2 percent for LoRA
versus 89.4 percent for FFT. Meanwhile, LoRA uses about 1.18 million trainable parameters per
adapter versus 125.8 million for full fine-tuning — that's 107 times fewer parameters.

This shows that the oracle-routed LoRA mixture matches standalone per-task LoRA exactly, because
the oracle router selects the right adapter for every example. More importantly, it demonstrates
the key Mixture-of-LoRA idea: a single shared base model plus a pool of six lightweight adapters
covers all six tasks simultaneously — with better macro performance than full fine-tuning and at
107 times lower parameter cost per task. The one place full fine-tuning clearly wins is on the
large, well-resourced tasks like MRPC and QQP where its extra capacity helps. But LoRA's big win
on RTE — a small 277-example dataset — shows that lightweight adapters also resist overfitting,
which is a meaningful practical advantage.


--- SLIDE: CAVEATS ---

Before I wrap up, a few honest caveats. All of this is a single-seed exploratory prototype, not a
rigorous multi-seed benchmark. The LoRA overlap analysis uses merged checkpoint deltas rather than
raw adapter matrices, which is a valid proxy but slightly less clean. The oracle router knows task
labels at inference time — a real deployment would need a learned classifier or embedding-based
router. And all numbers are from GLUE validation splits, not held-out test sets. With those
caveats stated, the core story holds: LoRA updates are geometrically structured, they partially
share the dominant directions of full fine-tuning, and a task-routed pool of lightweight adapters
is a practical path to a general multi-task model at a fraction of the fine-tuning cost.
